# Copywriting

The craft of writing that moves people to act.

## Canonical sources

- **David Ogilvy** — *Ogilvy on Advertising*, *Confessions of an Advertising Man*. The discipline of research-backed copy. Headline carries 80% of the weight. Long copy outperforms short copy for considered purchases. Test everything; instinct is not evidence.
- **Claude Hopkins** — *Scientific Advertising* (1923). The original data-driven copywriter. Every claim must be provable; every headline must offer something the reader wants. Most principles written here are still taught unchanged.
- **Eugene Schwartz** — *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966). The five stages of customer awareness. Mass desire exists before the product; the copywriter channels it, not creates it. One of the most influential copywriting books ever written.
- **Gary Halbert** — *The Boron Letters*. Direct-response mastery. Writing to one person, not a crowd. The "starving crowd" concept: the right audience matters more than the right copy.
- **Joseph Sugarman** — *The Adweek Copywriting Handbook*. The "slippery slide" — every sentence's only job is to get the reader to read the next sentence. Psychological triggers in copywriting.
- **Ann Handley** — *Everybody Writes*. Content-era update: good writing is a business asset, not a talent. Pathologically empathetic writing.

## Core frameworks

### AIDA
The foundational copywriting sequence (see `fundamentals.md`). Every piece of copy — ad, email, landing page, GitHub README — should follow this arc.

### PAS — Problem, Agitation, Solution
A shorter, higher-intensity alternative to AIDA. Works especially well for pain-driven products.
1. **Problem:** Name the specific problem the reader has right now.
2. **Agitation:** Deepen the pain. Make it vivid. Show what happens if the problem is not solved.
3. **Solution:** Present your product as the specific relief.

PAS is more visceral than AIDA. Use it when the problem is acute and well-known to the reader.

### The 4 U's — Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific
A headline evaluation checklist (from American Writers & Artists Institute). Score each U from 1–4. A strong headline scores 3–4 on all four. Most headlines are weak because they score 1–2 on Ultra-specific and Unique.

### Five Stages of Awareness (Schwartz)
The most important framework for matching copy to the reader's state of mind.

| Stage | Reader state | What to lead with |
|---|---|---|
| **Unaware** | Doesn't know they have a problem | A story, a world-view, a big truth — not the product |
| **Problem-aware** | Feels the pain, hasn't named a solution category | The pain, vividly named |
| **Solution-aware** | Knows solutions exist, hasn't found yours | The category and why this category is the answer |
| **Product-aware** | Knows your product, hasn't decided | Your differentiation and proof |
| **Most-aware** | Ready to buy | Offer, price, guarantee, CTA |

Writing product-aware copy for a problem-aware audience produces zero response. The most common copywriting mistake.

### The Slippery Slide (Sugarman)
Every sentence's only job is to pull the reader to the next sentence. The headline pulls them into the first sentence. The first sentence pulls them to the second. The CTA is where they arrive after sliding down. Any sentence that breaks the slide — by being boring, confusing, or demanding effort — loses them.

## Practical principles

### Headlines
- **The headline spends 80 cents of your dollar (Ogilvy).** If the headline fails, nothing else matters. Invest time accordingly.
- A good headline makes a specific promise or asks a question the reader urgently wants answered.
- Test headline variants before testing anything else in a page or ad.
- Specificity outperforms cleverness. "How I cut my AI retry rate from 2.3 to 0.7 in 30 days" beats "Work smarter with AI."
- Numbers in headlines outperform vague superlatives: "5 findings" beats "several surprising insights."

### Body copy
- **Sell benefits, not features.** A feature is what the product does; a benefit is what the reader gets. Feature: "local execution." Benefit: "your usage data never leaves your machine."
- **Write to one person, not a crowd.** Imagine the specific individual you are writing to. "You" singular, not "users."
- **Short sentences. Short paragraphs.** Long blocks of text are abandoned. Each paragraph should contain one idea.
- **Use the reader's exact language.** Phrases from customer interviews, support tickets, and community posts convert better than phrases you invent. The reader sees their own words and thinks "this is for me."
- **Anticipate and answer objections in the copy.** Every unaddressed objection is a reason to stop reading.
- **Proof before claim.** State the result first ("retry rate dropped from 2.3 to 0.7"), then explain how. Reversed order ("we built a system that...") buries the reason to care.

### Structure
- **One message per piece of copy.** One headline promise, one CTA. Multiple messages split attention and reduce conversion.
- **The CTA must match the desire level.** "Buy now" for an unaware audience fails. "See how it works" for a most-aware audience loses the conversion.
- **The P.S. in emails is the second-most-read element** after the subject line. Use it for the most important single point.

### Tone
- **Write at the level of your audience, not below it.** Developer audiences read quickly and detect condescension immediately.
- **Avoid corporate hedging.** "May potentially help improve" is not a claim. "Reduced retry rate by 67%" is a claim.
- **Ogilvy's rule:** "Never write an advertisement which you wouldn't want your family to read. You wouldn't tell lies to your own wife. Don't tell them to mine."
