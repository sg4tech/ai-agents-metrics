# Brand and Personal Brand

What a brand is, how equity is built, and how individuals build authority in a domain.

## Canonical sources

- **David Aaker** — *Building Strong Brands*, *Managing Brand Equity*. The foundational brand equity framework: brand awareness, brand loyalty, perceived quality, brand associations, proprietary assets. Aaker's model is a scorecard.
- **Kevin Lane Keller** — *Strategic Brand Management*. Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) model: brand identity → brand meaning → brand response → brand resonance. Keller's model is a map — it shows the journey from first impression to advocacy.
- **Marty Neumeier** — *The Brand Gap*, *Zag*. Brand is not a logo; it is a gut feeling. A brand is a person's gut feeling about a product, service, or company. The brand lives in the audience's head, not in the company's handbook.
- **Simon Sinek** — *Start With Why*. The Golden Circle: Why → How → What. People don't buy what you do; they buy why you do it. Brand belief system precedes product claims.
- **Ryan Holiday** — *Perennial Seller*. Building work — and a personal brand — that compounds over years rather than spiking and fading. The "platform" concept: own a space before you need it.
- **David Perell, Shawn Wang (Swyx)** — not books, but canonical practitioners of personal brand in the internet era. Perell: "Write in Public." Swyx: "Learn in Public." Both demonstrate that consistent, specific, original output over time builds authority that cannot be purchased.

## Core concepts

### What a brand actually is
A brand is the set of associations a person holds in their mind about an entity — a company, product, or person. It is not the logo, the name, the color palette, or the tagline. Those are brand assets. The brand itself lives in the audience's head.

**Brand equity** is the value those associations create: pricing power, trust shortcut, lower acquisition cost, customer advocacy. Strong brands command premium prices and generate word-of-mouth that weak brands cannot buy.

### Aaker's brand equity components
Five assets that constitute brand equity:
1. **Brand awareness.** Can the customer recall or recognize the brand in the category? Awareness is the prerequisite for everything else.
2. **Brand loyalty.** How committed are customers to repurchasing? Loyalty is the most defensible equity component — it takes years to build and competitors cannot copy it fast.
3. **Perceived quality.** What do customers believe about quality, relative to alternatives? Perception, not objective measurement.
4. **Brand associations.** Everything else the brand triggers in memory: values, personality, visual identity, spokesperson, usage situations.
5. **Proprietary brand assets.** Patents, trademarks, channel relationships — structural advantages.

### Keller's CBBE pyramid
The brand-building sequence from zero to advocacy:

| Level | Question | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **Identity** (Who are you?) | Brand salience | Is the brand recalled in the right situations? |
| **Meaning** (What are you?) | Performance + imagery | Does it deliver functionally? What does it say about the user? |
| **Response** (What do I think/feel?) | Judgements + feelings | Is the brand seen as credible, superior, relevant? |
| **Resonance** (What about you and me?) | Loyalty + community | Do customers advocate, belong, actively engage? |

Resonance is the peak. It is where customers market for you. It cannot be purchased directly; it is the result of consistently delivering on identity, meaning, and response over time.

### Personal brand
A personal brand is the set of associations an audience holds about an individual's expertise, point of view, and reliability. In technical communities (developer tools, AI, software engineering), personal brand follows the same mechanics as product brand — built by consistent, specific, original output over time.

**Core personal brand mechanics:**

- **Specificity beats generality.** "I write about developer productivity with AI coding agents" compounds faster than "I write about tech." Narrow domains have smaller audiences and higher trust.
- **POV is the differentiator.** Aggregated summaries have no brand value; anyone can summarize. A distinct point of view — even a controversial one — creates recall and advocacy.
- **Consistency over spikes.** One viral post creates a follower spike that fades in a week. Fifty good posts over a year build an audience that stays.
- **Write in public / learn in public (Perell / Swyx).** Publishing your actual learning process — including uncertainty, mistakes, and updates — is more credible than publishing polished conclusions. Technical audiences value epistemic honesty.
- **Own the platform before you need it.** Build an audience or publication before you have something to promote. Audiences built under urgency are thin; audiences built over time are loyal.

### Brand vs. reputation
- **Brand** is actively shaped: positioning, messaging, visual identity, consistent output.
- **Reputation** is earned passively: the sum of what others say about you when you're not in the room.

Great brands generate great reputations. But reputation cannot be manufactured — it is the output of consistent performance + honest communication over time.

## Practical principles

- **Brand is a promise kept consistently.** The promise is the positioning. The brand is what happens when you keep or break it over thousands of interactions.
- **Awareness before persuasion.** No amount of excellent copy works on someone who has never heard of you. Awareness is the prerequisite; it cannot be skipped.
- **Associations are easier to add than to remove.** Once a negative association attaches to a brand (cheap, unreliable, overhyped), removing it is extremely expensive. Don't make claims you can't consistently back.
- **For a developer tool, technical credibility is the primary brand asset.** A reputation for accuracy, correctness, and epistemic honesty builds faster and lasts longer than design polish or marketing spend.
- **One area of authority, deeply.** Personal brands built on broad, surface-level takes compound slowly. Personal brands built on one specific domain compound fast. Become the person people think of when they think of [specific thing].
- **Publish the real work, not the polished summary.** The analysis, the mistake, the unexpected finding — these build credibility. The press release and the launch post do not.
- **Brand equity is a lagging indicator.** You cannot see it building month-to-month. Measure proxy signals: unprompted mentions, inbound requests, being cited as a source by someone who didn't ask you first.
