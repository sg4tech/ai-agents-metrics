# Principles: Great vs Competent, Tensions, Anti-Patterns

Taste-level material. Not frameworks — judgments that distinguish a great marketer from a merely competent one.

## What distinguishes great marketers

1. **Starts with the customer's problem, not the product's features.** Can state the buyer's problem more precisely than the buyer can. Uses this to frame every piece of communication.
2. **Owns a metric.** Not "increase brand awareness." One number — conversion rate, qualified pipeline, NRR — with a target and a cadence.
3. **Uses customer language, not company language.** Finds the words customers use in support tickets, community posts, and interviews. Uses those exact phrases in copy.
4. **Separates positioning work from execution.** Knows that great copy written from bad positioning is still a waste. Never skips the positioning step to ship faster.
5. **Tests before scaling.** Runs the smallest possible test before committing budget. Does not scale a channel that has not been validated.
6. **Treats distribution as a first-class strategic question.** Does not assume the product will "speak for itself" or "spread naturally." Plans and resources distribution as seriously as product.
7. **Chooses one channel to master.** Does not spread across eight channels simultaneously. Goes deep on the highest-potential channel first.
8. **Knows what awareness level they're writing for.** Does not write problem-aware copy for a most-aware audience or vice versa. Matches the message to where the buyer actually is.
9. **Can write clearly.** Can compress a positioning statement into one sentence. Can explain a complex product to a stranger in two minutes. Writing ability is a proxy for clarity of thinking.
10. **Treats data as hypotheses, not conclusions.** Knows that a conversion lift in a test is a signal, not proof. Runs follow-up tests to confirm direction.

## Key tensions in the field

Tensions without settled answers. Hold the tradeoff rather than picking a side dogmatically.

- **Brand vs performance.** Brand investment builds long-term pricing power and reduces CAC over time. Performance marketing produces measurable short-term results. Companies that cut brand entirely in favor of performance often see CAC inflate in 18–24 months as brand equity depletes.
- **Product-led vs sales-led.** Not a binary. Most durable B2B products run both: PLG for SMB and discovery, sales for enterprise expansion. The mistake is treating it as ideology rather than segment economics.
- **Broad reach vs narrow ICP.** Targeting the smallest viable market feels like leaving money on the table. Targeting everyone produces generic messaging that resonates with no one. The narrow ICP produces word-of-mouth; the broad target does not.
- **Category creation vs category entry.** Creating a new category is higher ceiling but requires educating the market on both the problem and the solution. Entering an existing category is faster to customer understanding but requires differentiation in a crowded frame. Neither is universally right.
- **Authenticity vs polish.** Developer communities (and increasingly, many B2B buyers) distrust polished marketing. Raw, honest, technically credible content often outperforms professional creative. But "authentic" is not a substitute for clear or useful.
- **Short-term conversion vs long-term trust.** High-pressure tactics (urgency, dark patterns, aggressive follow-up) can improve short-term conversion while degrading brand trust and NRR. The tradeoff is invisible in 30-day metrics and obvious in 18-month cohorts.

## Anti-patterns

- **Launching before a finding.** Shipping to influential channels without a concrete, specific result to share. Produces noise, not signal. One-shot channels (HN, a big newsletter) are wasted.
- **Positioning written by the founding team, never tested.** The words the team uses internally are not the words customers use. Untested positioning is hypothesis, not truth.
- **"Build it and they will come."** The assumption that product quality alone drives distribution. It does not. Distribution is a separate problem requiring separate investment.
- **Superlatives and platitudes.** "Best in class," "revolutionary," "seamless," "powerful." Readers discount these automatically. Specific facts ("reduces retry rate from 2.3 to 0.7") are believed.
- **Feature announcements as marketing.** "We shipped feature X" is interesting to existing users. It is not a message for prospects, who do not yet understand what value X creates.
- **Marketing to the whole market.** Targeting everyone produces messaging that resonates with no one. The instinct to not exclude anyone is the single most common early-stage marketing mistake.
- **Measuring activity instead of outcomes.** "We published 12 posts," "we sent 3 DMs," "we ran 2 campaigns." Activity metrics are not pipeline or revenue. Track conversion, not output.
- **Sprinting on a channel without validating it first.** Investing heavily in a channel before the Bullseye test confirms it works. Most channels don't work for any given product; find the one that does before scaling.
- **Copying the incumbent's channel.** The incumbent owns that channel. Finding a channel they cannot easily replicate is more defensible than fighting them on their home ground.
- **Treating the README as an afterthought.** For OSS developer tools, the README is the highest-leverage marketing asset. A weak README kills otherwise good tools.
- **Social proof without specificity.** "Thousands of developers use this." Weaker than one named, credible developer saying one specific thing about a specific result.
