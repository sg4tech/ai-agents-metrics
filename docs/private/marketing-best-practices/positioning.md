# Positioning and Messaging

How customers understand what you are and why they should care.

## Canonical sources

- **April Dunford** — *Obviously Awesome*, *Sales Pitch*. The only book that treats positioning as a teachable craft with a repeatable process. Five components: competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value, best-fit customers, market category.
- **Al Ries & Jack Trout** — *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind*. The original. Positioning happens in the customer's mind, not in your product. The first mover in a category owns the position; everyone else fights for second.
- **Geoffrey Moore** — *Crossing the Chasm*. The chasm between early adopters (buy on vision) and early majority (buy on references). Whole Product concept: the complete solution a mainstream customer requires.
- **April Dunford** — *Sales Pitch*. Companion to *Obviously Awesome*: how to translate positioning into a sales narrative. The "context setting" opening: frame what you are before you describe what you do.
- **Ryan Holiday** — *Trust Me, I'm Lying*. The other side: how media narratives are constructed and weaponized. Useful for understanding what makes a story spread.

## Core frameworks

- **Dunford's five components.** Positioning = (1) competitive alternatives — what would customers use if you didn't exist? (2) unique attributes — what do you uniquely provide? (3) value — why do those attributes matter to customers? (4) best-fit customers — who values this most? (5) market category — what context makes the value obvious?
- **Positioning → Messaging → Copy.** Strict order. Positioning is the internal strategic document. Messaging translates it into audience-facing themes. Copy is the execution. Skipping levels produces generic text.
- **Category design (Ries & Trout / Dunford).** If no category fits, create one. Owning a new category is more defensible than competing in an established one. But the category name must be recognizable to the buyer — invented jargon fails.
- **Whole Product (Moore).** The product itself is not what the mainstream buyer purchases. They purchase the complete solution: product + integrations + services + community + support. Marketing must reflect the whole product, not just the core feature.
- **The Chasm.** Early adopters accept incomplete products for competitive advantage. Early majority want proof, references, and a complete solution. The gap between them is the chasm. Crossing it requires a "beachhead" — one vertical where you are the complete solution.

## Practical principles

- **Positioning before copy, always.** Writing headlines before positioning is finished produces superlatives and platitudes. Positioning answers "what are you." Copy is translation.
- **One category, clearly.** If customers need a paragraph to understand the category, positioning has failed. One phrase. Test it with a stranger.
- **Competitive alternatives are set by the customer, not by you.** Your competitor is whatever the buyer uses today — including "do nothing" and spreadsheets.
- **Lead with the context, not the product.** Buyers need a frame before they can evaluate a claim. Set the context first, then describe what you do.
- **Unique ≠ better.** A unique attribute only matters if it produces a value the customer cares about. "Local-first" is a unique attribute; "private by design" is the value.
- **Positioning is a document, not a tagline.** The tagline is the compression of positioning, not its replacement.
- **Reframe, don't fight.** If you're in a crowded category, create a sub-category or a new frame rather than competing on the same attributes as incumbents.
