# Psychology and Persuasion

How human decision-making works and how marketing accounts for it.

## Canonical sources

- **Robert Cialdini** — *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*, *Pre-Suasion*. Seven principles of influence: reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity, unity. *Pre-Suasion*: the frame set before the message matters as much as the message.
- **Daniel Kahneman** — *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. System 1 (fast, intuitive, heuristic) vs System 2 (slow, analytical, deliberate). Most purchase decisions are System 1. Marketing that requires System 2 effort loses.
- **David Ogilvy** — *Ogilvy on Advertising*, *Confessions of an Advertising Man*. The original data-driven advertiser. More facts → higher response. Headlines must pay rent. Research wins over instinct.
- **Eugene Schwartz** — *Breakthrough Advertising*. The five stages of customer awareness: unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware, most-aware. Copy must match the awareness level of the audience.
- **Claude Hopkins** — *Scientific Advertising*. The discipline of testing and measurement in advertising, written in 1923. Foundational.
- **Rory Sutherland** — *Alchemy: The Dark Art and Curious Science of Creating Magic in Brands*. Behavioural economics applied to marketing. Value is perception, not utility. Irrational interventions often outperform rational ones.

## Core concepts

- **Seven principles of influence (Cialdini):**
  1. **Reciprocity.** Give first — people feel compelled to return the favour. Free content, free tools, free analysis.
  2. **Commitment / consistency.** People want to act consistently with what they've already said or done. Small yeses lead to larger yeses.
  3. **Social proof.** People follow others in uncertainty. Reviews, citations, user counts, named endorsers.
  4. **Authority.** Credentials, track records, and institutional affiliation reduce purchase anxiety.
  5. **Liking.** People buy from those they like. Similarity, genuine interest in the buyer, and shared identity increase liking.
  6. **Scarcity.** Perceived rarity increases perceived value. Real scarcity is more powerful than artificial scarcity.
  7. **Unity.** Shared identity ("we are the same kind of people") is a deeper driver than social proof. Community and tribe over crowd.

- **System 1 vs System 2 (Kahneman).** Most marketing encounters happen in System 1 mode. If comprehension requires effort, the prospect moves on. Clarity and simplicity are not dumbing down — they are respect for cognitive load.

- **Five awareness levels (Schwartz).** Marketing copy must match where the prospect is in their awareness journey:
  - *Unaware* — doesn't know they have a problem. Lead with the world, not the product.
  - *Problem-aware* — knows the pain, hasn't named a solution category yet. Lead with the pain.
  - *Solution-aware* — knows solutions exist, doesn't know yours. Lead with the category.
  - *Product-aware* — knows your product, hasn't decided. Lead with differentiation.
  - *Most-aware* — ready to buy, just needs a nudge. Lead with offer and price.

- **Ogilvy's rules (from *Ogilvy on Advertising*).** The headline carries 80% of the weight. Facts outperform clever. Superlatives ("best," "revolutionary") are discounted by readers; specific facts are believed. Long copy sells better than short copy for considered purchases. Test everything.

## Practical principles

- **Lead with reciprocity.** Give the real thing for free — analysis, insight, tool. Not a watered-down teaser. The more useful the free offering, the stronger the obligation dynamic.
- **Name the problem before the solution.** Most marketing leads with the product. Most buyers don't yet have a named solution category in mind. Lead with the pain first.
- **Social proof = named people, not counts.** "Used by 3000 developers" is weaker than "Simon Willison uses it for X." Named, credible individuals outperform anonymous counts.
- **Match the awareness level.** Writing to a problem-aware audience as if they are product-aware (jumping to features) produces zero response. The copy must meet them where they are.
- **Specificity beats superlatives.** "Retry rate dropped from 2.3 to 0.7 in 30 days" is believed. "Dramatically improves AI efficiency" is not. Ogilvy: facts win.
- **The frame before the content (Cialdini's Pre-Suasion).** What you say before the main message shapes how it is received. Set the context — "I'm going to show you something most developers overlook" — before the claim.
- **Don't fight System 1.** Make the value obvious in three seconds or lose. A technical audience is not immune to cognitive load; they are just impatient for clarity.
- **Ethical application only.** Influence principles describe how humans work; they can be used to help people make decisions they will not regret, or to manipulate. The distinguishing test: would the buyer, after purchase, feel they were helped or tricked?
