# B2B and Developer Marketing

How to market to businesses, technical buyers, and developer communities.

## Canonical sources

- **Elena Verna** — PLG motion, self-serve B2B, growth loops in product-led companies. The "free-to-paid" conversion mechanics. Viral loops in B2B.
- **Kyle Poyar** (OpenView) — PLG benchmarks, pricing research, the PLG-to-sales handoff. Annual PLG benchmark reports.
- **Sangram Vajre** — *Account-Based Marketing for Dummies*, *MOVE*. ABM: treat each target account as a market of one. Alignment between marketing and sales on a shared target account list.
- **David Sacks** — the SaaS metrics canon: CAC, LTV, payback period, net revenue retention (NRR). Go-to-market fit as a concept distinct from product-market fit.
- **Adam Schoenfeld & Wynter** — B2B message testing at scale. Customer language research: the words customers use to describe their own problem are more persuasive than the words you invent for them.
- **Patrick Campbell** (ProfitWell / Paddle) — B2B SaaS pricing research corpus. Value-based pricing in practice.
- **Adam Wathan & Steve Schoger** — *Refactoring UI*. Indirect reference: developer-product marketing works when the product speaks for itself. Developer audiences respond to demonstrated competence, not claimed benefits.

## Core concepts

- **ICP (Ideal Customer Profile).** Not a demographic. A precise description of the account type that converts fastest, retains longest, and expands most reliably. Defined by: firmographics (size, industry, stage), technographics (tools in use), behaviours (how they spend, how they decide), and fit (LTV, ACV, CAC ratio). ICP is the anchor for every channel and messaging decision.
- **PLG motion.** The product is the primary distribution vehicle. Acquisition happens through usage, not sales. Requires: short time-to-value, value demonstrable without a rep, natural viral spread (one user shows another). Works for SMB and discovery; sales augments at enterprise tail.
- **PLG-to-sales handoff.** The product qualifies leads (product-qualified leads, PQLs) through usage signals. Sales engages accounts that are already activated, not cold. The handoff triggers on: account size, usage depth, or expansion signal.
- **Developer marketing ≠ B2C marketing.** Developers are resistant to marketing that looks like marketing. They respond to: demonstrated technical competence, real results from real usage, peer endorsement, and tools that save real time. Developer advocates and community members who use the product authentically are more effective than polished campaigns.
- **Message testing (Wynter / customer language research).** The language your ICP uses to describe their problem is more persuasive than the language you invent. Customer interviews, support tickets, and community posts are the raw material. Mining these for exact phrases produces copy that resonates immediately.
- **NRR (Net Revenue Retention) as the marketing north star.** In B2B SaaS, the best marketing is a product that expands within accounts. NRR > 120% means the existing customer base grows on its own. Marketing's job is not just acquisition — it is onboarding, activation, and expansion that support NRR.
- **Community-led growth.** Communities compound. A strong user community provides: social proof, peer support (reducing CS costs), feedback loops, and organic distribution. Works when the community has a shared identity and shared problems.

## Practical principles — developer tools specifically

- **Show, don't tell.** A working demo or real output beats a feature list every time. The GitHub README animated GIF that shows the tool working in 10 seconds converts better than a marketing page.
- **Lead with a finding, not a feature.** "I ran this tool on 30 days of my AI coding history and found X" outperforms "this tool has feature Y." The finding proves the value; the feature only describes it.
- **Analysis posts >> product posts.** In developer communities (HN, Reddit, Twitter/X), posts structured as "I measured X and found Y — here's the methodology" spread organically. Posts structured as "I built a tool" generate much less engagement.
- **Peer endorsement > company claim.** Developer audiences weight "developer X I respect uses this" above any marketing copy. Targeting specific influential individuals is higher leverage than broad campaigns.
- **The README is the landing page.** For OSS developer tools, the README is the first and often only marketing surface. It must: (1) state the value in one sentence, (2) show the tool working in 30 seconds, (3) answer "who is this for," (4) make installation trivial. Everything else is secondary.
- **One-shot channels require pre-qualification.** Show HN, a big Twitter thread, a newsletter feature — these are high-reach, one-time events. Launching on them before you have social proof wastes the shot. Wait until you have independent citations or a finding worth sharing.
- **Use customer language in positioning.** The words your users use about their problem are the words to use in headlines, not the words that feel right to the builder.
